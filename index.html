Rib Fractures

Rib fractures occur when a significant enough force directed at the rib causes a break.

Rib Fracture Imaging Visualization

Clinical Insight: Rib fractures are often associated with other injuries. The greater the number of rib fractures, the more likely associated injuries occur. Rib fractures can occur at any age, but older people are at higher risk because of osteoporosis.

Key Patterns

  • Fractures of the 4th-10th ribs are the most common
  • Fractures of the 1st-3rd ribs are associated with high-energy trauma
  • When a rib is fractured twice, it's termed a "floating rib"
  • Three or more contiguous floating ribs are called a "flail chest"

Physiotherapy Management

Pain Management

Pharmacological and non-pharmacological approaches to control pain and facilitate breathing

Breathing Exercises

Techniques to prevent complications and additional damage to lungs

Early Mobilization

Gradual progression of movement to prevent respiratory complications

Flail Chest

Flail chest occurs when a segment of the rib cage breaks due to blunt thoracic trauma and becomes unattached from the chest wall.

Clinical Insight: It can occur when 3 or more ribs are broken in at least two places, although not everyone with this type of injury will develop a flail chest.

Types

  • Complete
  • Incomplete

Pulmonary Complications (48-72 hours after admission)

Haemothorax

Accumulation of blood in the pleural cavity

Pneumothorax

Air in the pleural space causing lung collapse

Atelectasis

Partial or complete lung collapse

Pleural Effusion

Excess fluid buildup in the pleural space

Subcutaneous Emphysema

Air trapped under the skin

ARDS

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Physiotherapy Management

  • Pain Management and Education: Crucial for effective breathing
  • Chest and Airway Clearance Techniques: To maintain open airways
  • Deep Breathing Exercises: Supported coughing techniques
  • Positioning: Optimal positions for ventilation and secretion clearance
Pneumothorax

A pneumothorax is defined as air in the pleural cavity, occurring when there is a breach of the lung surface or chest wall allowing air to enter the pleural cavity and cause lung collapse.

Types of Pneumothorax

Type Description Characteristics
Primary Pneumothorax Spontaneous with no clear cause No known underlying lung pathology
Secondary Pneumothorax Non-spontaneous or complicated Result of underlying lung pathology (COPD, Asthma, TB, etc.)

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Spontaneous

  • Gender (more common in males)
  • Smoking (tobacco or cannabis)
  • Family history

Secondary Spontaneous

  • Injury or trauma
  • COPD
  • Cystic fibrosis
  • Lung cancer
  • Asthma
  • Tuberculosis
  • Bacterial pneumonia

Hemothorax

Hemothorax is defined as the entry of pleural fluid and blood into the pleural cavity, with a hematocrit of 25-50% of the patient's blood.

Hemothorax Imaging Visualization

Clinical Presentation

  • Chest pain
  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath)
  • Fever
  • Tachycardia (increased heart rate)
  • Reduced breath sounds on the affected side
  • Pallor
  • Cold sweats

Diagnostic Procedures

Chest X-ray

Initial imaging to detect fluid in pleural space

Ultrasound

Bedside assessment for fluid detection

CT Scan

Detailed imaging for complex cases

MRI Scan

Alternative imaging with no radiation

Physiotherapy Management

The patient's clinical picture should guide treatment decisions:

  • Treatment differs for patients with chest tubes vs. post-surgical
  • Deep breathing exercises to improve ventilation and oxygenation
  • Early mobilization to improve exercise tolerance
  • Assisted coughing techniques for airway clearance
  • Re-inflation of atelectatic lung areas
Lung Injuries

Clinical Insight: Lung contusion is essentially a bruise on the lung caused by blunt force trauma, while lung laceration involves a tear or cut in the lung tissue itself.

Lung Contusion

  • Caused by blunt force trauma (MVA, falls, sports injuries)
  • Leads to bleeding and fluid accumulation without tearing
  • Damages small blood vessels in the lungs

Symptoms

  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Coughing (possibly with blood)
  • Wheezing
  • Rapid breathing
  • Cyanosis (late sign)

Lung Laceration

  • Caused by penetrating trauma or severe blunt trauma
  • Disrupts structural integrity of the lung
  • May lead to pneumothorax or hemothorax

Symptoms

  • Sudden chest pain
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Coughing up blood
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Signs of shock if significant bleeding

Airway Clearance

  • Percussion and vibration
  • Forced expiratory techniques

Breathing Exercises

  • Diaphragmatic breathing
  • Incentive spirometry
  • Segmental breathing

Mobilization

  • Early ambulation
  • Progressive exercise
  • Positioning techniques
Pneumonia

Pneumonia refers to an infection of the lung parenchyma, characterized by inflammation and consolidation of lung tissue.

Classification

By Infective Agent

  • Bacterial (most common cause)
  • Viral (e.g., influenza, COVID-19)
  • Fungal (in immunocompromised)

By Setting

  • Community-acquired (CAP)
  • Hospital-acquired (HAP)
  • Ventilator-associated (VAP)
  • Aspiration pneumonia

By Pathology

  • Bronchopneumonia
  • Lobar pneumonia

Signs and Symptoms

  • Chest pain when breathing or coughing
  • Confusion (especially in elderly)
  • Cough with phlegm production
  • Fatigue and fever with shaking chills
  • Lower than normal body temperature
  • Nausea, vomiting or diarrhea
  • Shortness of breath

Physiotherapy Management

Comprehensive approach to pneumonia management:

Secretion Clearance

  • Active Cycle of Breathing
  • Autogenic Drainage
  • Positive Expiratory Pressure

Breathing Techniques

  • Controlling respiratory rate
  • Diaphragmatic breathing
  • Relaxation breathing

Positioning

  • Postural drainage
  • Positions of ease
  • Paced breathing during exercise
Empyema

Empyema is a collection of pus in the space between the lung and the inner surface of the chest wall (pleural space).

Empyema Imaging Visualization

Clinical Insight: Empyema typically results from bacterial pneumonia, tuberculosis, chest surgery, lung abscess, or trauma to the chest.

Risk Factors

  • Bacterial pneumonia
  • Tuberculosis
  • Chest surgery
  • Lung abscess
  • Trauma or injury to the chest

Symptoms

Respiratory

Chest pain, dry cough, shortness of breath

Systemic

Fever and chills, excessive sweating (night sweats)

General

General discomfort, malaise, unintentional weight loss

Atelectasis

Atelectasis is the partial collapse or closure of a lung resulting in reduced or absence in gas exchange. It is usually unilateral, affecting part or all of one lung.

Atelectasis Visualization

Symptoms

  • Cough (not prominent)
  • Chest pain (not common)
  • Breathing difficulty (fast and shallow)
  • Low oxygen saturation
  • Pleural effusion (transudate type)
  • Cyanosis (late sign)
  • Increased heart rate

Physiotherapy Management

  • Deep breathing exercises
  • Encouraging coughing
  • Incentive spirometer use
  • Early ambulation and walking
  • Positioning to optimize ventilation
  • Chest physiotherapy techniques
Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a genetic disease that causes sticky, thick mucus to build up in your body. This can damage your lungs, pancreas and other organs.

Hemoptysis

Hemoptysis or haemoptysis is the discharge of blood or blood-stained mucus through the mouth coming from the bronchi, larynx, trachea, or lungs.

Clinical Significance

Hemoptysis can range from mild to life-threatening. Massive hemoptysis (>500 ml in 24 hours) is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention.

Causes

  • Bronchitis
  • Lung cancer
  • Tuberculosis
  • Pneumonia
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Lung abscess

Assessment

  • Determine amount and duration of bleeding
  • Assess for signs of respiratory distress
  • Check vital signs including oxygen saturation
  • Evaluate for associated symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • Review medical history and risk factors
Bronchiectasis

Bronchiectasis is a condition that occurs when the tubes that carry air in and out of your lungs get damaged, causing them to widen and become loose and scarred.

Lung Abscess

Lung abscess is a type of infection that occurs in the lungs, characterized by a collection of pus in a cavity within the lung tissue.

Clinical Insight: Lung abscesses are typically caused by aspiration of oral secretions in patients with impaired consciousness or dysphagia, leading to polymicrobial infection.

Causes

  • Bacterial infection (most common)
  • Fungal infection
  • Parasitic infection
  • Aspiration of foreign material
  • Obstruction of bronchus
  • Septic embolism

Symptoms

Respiratory

Productive cough with foul-smelling sputum, hemoptysis

Systemic

Fever, night sweats, fatigue

General

Chest pain, shortness of breath, weight loss, clubbing

Tuberculosis

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis that most often affects the lungs.

  • Fever and night sweats
  • Fatigue

Asthma is a disease characterized by an increased responsiveness of the trachea and bronchi to various stimuli, manifested by narrowing airways.

Pulmonary Embolism

Pulmonary Embolism (PE) is a life-threatening condition that occurs when one or more arteries in the lungs become blocked by a blood clot.

Clinical Significance

PE is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment. Massive PE can cause sudden death.

Causes

  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
  • Recent surgery (especially orthopedic or abdominal)
  • Trauma
  • Cancer
  • Genetic disorders (Factor V Leiden)
  • Prolonged immobility

Symptoms

Respiratory

Sudden shortness of breath, pleuritic chest pain, cough

Cardiovascular

Tachycardia, hypotension, syncope, cyanosis

Systemic

Anxiety, fever, diaphoresis, lightheadedness

CPR Techniques

Life-Saving Technique

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is used to restore blood circulation and breathing in a person who has stopped breathing or whose heart has stopped beating.

Adult CPR

  1. Call emergency services
  2. Check airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs)
  3. Start compressions (2-3 inches depth, 100-120/min)
  4. Give rescue breaths (2 breaths after 30 compressions)
  5. Continue until help arrives or signs of consciousness

Child CPR (1-8 years)

  1. Call emergency services
  2. Check ABCs
  3. Start compressions (1/3 chest depth)
  4. Give rescue breaths (2 breaths after 30 compressions)
  5. Continue until help arrives

Infant CPR (0-12 months)

  1. Call emergency services
  2. Check ABCs
  3. Start compressions (1/4 chest depth with thumbs)
  4. Give rescue breaths (2 gentle breaths after 30 compressions)
  5. Continue until help arrives

Ventilators

Ventilators are life-support machines that assist patients in breathing when they are unable to do so on their own.

Types of Ventilators

Volume-Controlled

Delivers set tidal volume regardless of pressure

Pressure-Controlled

Delivers set pressure with each breath

Time-Cycled

Delivers breaths at a set rate

Flow-Cycled

Delivers breaths based on set flow rate

Modes of Ventilation

Mode Description Clinical Use
CMV Controlled Mechanical Ventilation Ventilator delivers all breaths
ACV Assist-Control Ventilation Set rate with patient-triggered breaths
SIMV Synchronized Intermittent Mandatory Ventilation Set rate with spontaneous breathing
PSV Pressure Support Ventilation Supports patient's spontaneous breaths

Principles of Ventilator Management

  • Oxygenation: Adjust FiO2 and PEEP for adequate oxygenation
  • Ventilation: Adjust tidal volume, respiratory rate, and inspiratory time
  • Monitoring: Continuous vital sign assessment
  • Weaning: Gradual reduction of support

Post-MI Management

Post-Myocardial Infarction (MI) management involves comprehensive rehabilitation strategies to optimize recovery, prevent complications, and improve quality of life for cardiac patients.

Cardiac Rehabilitation Visualization

Clinical Insight: Early mobilization and structured rehabilitation significantly reduce mortality and improve functional capacity in post-MI patients. The rehabilitation program should be tailored to individual patient needs and risk factors.

Goals of Post-MI Physiotherapy

Improve Cardiovascular Function

Enhance cardiac output, reduce cardiac workload, and promote cardiovascular stability

Enhance Respiratory Function

Improve lung expansion, increase oxygenation, and prevent respiratory complications

Prevent Muscle Weakness

Maintain muscle strength through early mobilization and targeted exercises

Improve Quality of Life

Promote physical activity, independence, and psychological well-being

Phases of Cardiac Rehabilitation

1 Phase I: Inpatient

  • Begin within 24-48 hours post-MI
  • Bed exercises and sitting tolerance
  • Breathing exercises and coughing techniques
  • Progressive ambulation
  • Education on risk factors

2 Phase II: Early Outpatient

  • Begin 2-6 weeks post-discharge
  • Supervised exercise program
  • Low-to-moderate intensity aerobic exercise
  • Resistance training introduction
  • Continued education and counseling

3 Phase III: Intermediate Outpatient

  • Months 3-6 post-MI
  • Progressive exercise intensity
  • Increased duration and frequency
  • Advanced resistance training
  • Lifestyle modification reinforcement

4 Phase IV: Maintenance

  • Long-term maintenance program
  • Community-based exercise
  • Ongoing risk factor management
  • Periodic reassessment
  • Support group participation

Physiotherapy Interventions

Aerobic Exercise

  • Walking, cycling, treadmill
  • Initial intensity: 40-60% HR max
  • Duration: 20-40 minutes
  • Frequency: 3-5 days/week

Resistance Training

  • Begin 3-4 weeks post-MI
  • Light weights (30-40% 1RM)
  • Focus on major muscle groups
  • 2-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions

Respiratory Training

  • Diaphragmatic breathing
  • Incentive spirometry
  • Pursed-lip breathing
  • Segmental breathing exercises

Precautions and Contraindications

Exercise Termination Criteria

Immediately stop exercise if patient experiences: Chest pain, severe dyspnea, dizziness, drop in SBP â‰¥10mmHg, significant arrhythmias, or ST-segment depression â‰¥2mm.

Absolute Contraindications to Exercise

  • Unstable angina
  • Resting systolic BP >200mmHg or diastolic BP >110mmHg
  • Orthostatic BP drop >20mmHg with symptoms
  • Severe aortic stenosis
  • Acute systemic illness or fever
  • Uncontrolled atrial or ventricular arrhythmias
